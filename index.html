Becoming a Town – Stadtwerdung

In the tenth century, on the site of a settlement - “buttle” in Old German - named after its founder Wulfheri, a water castle was built, surrounded by the River Oker. It bore the name “Wlferesbutle”. After the Wolfenbüttel dynasty, first documented in 1118, failed to hold its ground against the Welfs, the castle fell into the possession of the Welf dukes of Brunswick (Braunschweig)-Lüneburg in 1255. Starting in 1432 they expanded the “Dammfeste”, the fortified castle of Wolfenbüttel and residential area surrounding it, to make it their permanent residence. Towards the end of the 16th century, by ducal decree, the Heinrichsstadt district was built outside the gates of Wolfenbüttel. The districts Neue Heinrichsstadt, Gotteslager district, later Juliusstadt, and Auguststadt joined it.

Although Heinrichsstadt would gain market rights and the right to a coat of arms through imperial privilege, one cannot speak of the town of Wolfenbüttel as a whole until 1747. In that year, the name, which until then had referred only to the “Dammfeste”, was extended to include all parts of the town, and regulations harmonised in all the settled districts. Shortly after this consolidation as a town, in 1753/54, Duke Karl I moved the court back to Brunswick. For Wolfenbüttel, the long path from ducal town to civil society began.
